  • limits: rules
    Limits
    A limit is the process to find how a function's values become as it approaches a certain value. Here is the notation:
    $$\lim_{x \rightarrow a}f(x)$$
    Which is read as "the limit as x approaches a of $f(x)$".
    One sided limits
    In addition to finding regular limits, you can find a limit of a value when the function approaches it from the right or left. Finding the limit of a value "from the right" means to find what it is approaching from the $+x$ direction. To the contrary, finding the limit of a value "from the left" means to find what it is from the $-x$ direction. These differences are denoted by a $^+$ and $^-$ superscript: $$\lim_{x \rightarrow a^+}f(x)$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow a^-}f(x)$$ In this case, it is read as "the limit as x approaches a from the right / left of $f(x)$" respectively.
    Finding a limit graphically
    We know from the previous sections, we have limits, right sided limits, and left sided limits. To find them graphically, we merely look at what the function approaches.

    Finding a limit algebraically
    We know from the previous sections, we have limits, right sided limits, and left sided limits which are denoted by $\lim_{x \rightarrow a}f(x)$, $\lim_{x \rightarrow a^+}f(x)$, and $\lim_{x \rightarrow a^-}f(x)$ respectively. In order to find the limit algebraically, we merely plug in the value.

    • If the limit is well defined (no undefined $\frac{1}{0}$ or indeterminate $\frac{0}{0}$ values) then the limit exists. If the limit gives an undefined value, then the limit does not exist (DNE). Whereas, if the limit gives an indeterminate value, you must further simplify the value (factoring, cancelling, etc.).
    APPLY. Evaluate:
    $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 2} \frac{x^2+9}{5x-9}\ \text{Start with equation.}\\[5px]$$ $$=\lim_{x \rightarrow 2} \frac{(2)^2+9}{5(2)-9}$$ $$=\lim_{x \rightarrow 2} \frac{4+9}{10-9}$$ $$=\lim_{x \rightarrow 2} 13$$
    APPLY. Evalutate:
    $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{x^2+x}{3x}$$ $$=\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{x(x+1)}{3x}$$ $$=\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{x+1}{3}$$ $$=\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{1}{3}$$

    Determining continuity
    If a function is continuous, then it has no asymptotes (essential discontinuity), jumps, or removable discontinuities. The basic rule of thumb to follow is that if the graph can be made without lifting your pencil, then it is continuous. If not, it is said to be discontinuous. To determine whether, for example, $f(x)$ is continuous at $x=a$, you must test the existence of:
    • $$f(a)$$
    • $$\lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x)$$
    • $$f(a)=\lim_{x \rightarrow a} f(x)$$
    In other words, you must test to see if the function exists at $a$, if the limit exists as $x$ approaches $a$ and if the function at $a$ is equal to the limit as $x$ approaches $a$.
    APPLY. Determine if the function is continuous at x=2: $$f(x)=\frac{x^2-1}{x-2}$$ $$f(x)=\frac{(x+2)(x-2)}{x-2}$$ $$f(x)=x+2$$
    $$f(x)=x+2$$ $$f(2)=(2)+2$$ $$f(2)=4$$
    $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 2} x+2$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 2} (2)+2=4$$
    $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 2} = f(2) = 4$$
    Since the function meets all the requirements at x=2, the function is continuous.

    Continuity involving piecewise functions
    In order to determine continuity of a piecewise function, you must evaluate its left and right side limits and whether it exists at the point. If $\lim_{x \rightarrow a^-}f(x) = \lim_{x \rightarrow a^+}f(x)$ and the point exists then the piecewise function is continuous.
    APPLY. Determine if the piecewise function is continuous: $$f(x)=\begin{cases}x^2 & x \leq 0\\x+2 & x > 0\end{cases}$$
    $$f(x)=x^2$$ $$f(0)=(0)^2$$ $$f(0)=0$$
    $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} x+2$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} (0)+2=2$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} 2$$
    $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0^-} x^2$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0^-} (0)^2$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0^-} 0$$
    $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0^+} \ne \lim_{x \rightarrow 0^-}$$
    Since the limit as $x$ approaches $0$ from the right does not equal the limit as $x$ approaches $0$ from the left, then this piecewise function is not continuous at the point $x=0$.
  • limits: trig
    Trigonometric limits
    In addition to taking the limits of polynomials, piecewises, rationals, etc. we can take the limit of trigonometric functions. Here are some important identities, part of the $Sandwich \ Theorem$ (or the Squeeze Theorem):
    • $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{sinx}{x}=1$$
    • $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{x}{sinx}=1$$
    Since the value is negligable as we approach zero, the sandwich theorem is an identity we can use.
    APPLY. Evaluate: $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{sin^2x}{x}$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{sinx}{x} \times sinx$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} 1 \times sinx$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} sin(0)$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} 0$$
    Trigonometric limits using 1
    Sometimes we need to manipulate the trig function in order to solve the limit. One method is to multiply the function by a value equal to 1.
    APPLY. Evaluate: $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{sin2x}{sin3x}$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{sin2x}{sin3x} \times \frac{2x}{2x} \times \frac{3x}{3x}$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{sin2x}{2x} \times \frac{3x}{sin3x} \times \frac{2x}{3x}$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} 1 \times 1 \times \frac{2}{3}$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow 0} \frac{2}{3}$$
    Trigonometric limits using conjugates
    As stated before, we must use other methods in order to solve for the limit; another way is to use the conjugate.
    APPLY. Evaluate: $$\lim_{x \rightarrow \theta} \frac{\theta^2}{1-cos\theta}$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow \theta} \frac{\theta^2}{1-cos\theta} \times \frac{1+cos\theta}{1+cos\theta}$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow \theta} \frac{\theta^2 (1+cos\theta)}{(1-cos^2\theta)}$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow \theta} \frac{\theta^2}{sin^2\theta} \times 1+cos\theta$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow \theta} 1 \times 1+cos(0)$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow \theta} 1 + 1$$ $$\lim_{x \rightarrow \theta} 2$$
  • limits: infinity
    Limits involving infinity
    If we take the limit of a function and the denominator is zero, we can define:
    • $$\lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} \frac{-1}{0}=-\infty$$
    • $$\lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} \frac{1}{0}=\infty$$
    Since the value as we divide one by a very big number is very small, we can define the limit in this way.

    If we want to find the limit as $x$ approaches $\infty$ of a polynomial, we take the order of magnitude. $$\lim_{x \rightarrow \infty} \frac{ax^n-bx}{ux^n-c}=\frac{a}{u}$$ We can focus on the largest term since the other values are negligable. Thus, we can divide the largest term of the numerator by the largest term of the denominator.
